Assumptions & Beginner Blindspots Guide

The Financial Finish Line (Dynamic FIRE Number)

Think of this number as your financial finish line. Because things cost more in the future (inflation) and you may eventually pay off large debts (mortgages), the finish line moves every single year.

Instead of plotting a static target, this engine plots your FIRE Requirement as a dynamic, dotted curve on the chart. It calculates exactly how much money you need for any given year based on that year's specific inflation and remaining debt. Once your compounding portfolio (the green area) catches up and crosses above the dotted red line, you have achieved Financial Independence.

🛡️ The CPF LIFE "Margin of Safety"

This simulator deliberately excludes CPF LIFE payouts and the Age 55 Retirement Account (RA) deduction. We recommend treating your eventual CPF LIFE payouts as a pure "margin of safety" rather than relying on it to fund your base FIRE calculations. For conceptual planning, the 2026 CPF cohort benchmarks (in today's dollars) are roughly $110,200 yielding ~$900/mo (BRS) or $220,400 yielding ~$1,700/mo (FRS).

Historical Global Returns & Margin of Safety

When projecting global equity returns, history offers a guide. Over the last 20-30 years, US-only indices (like the S&P 500) have annualized roughly 10-10.5%, while globally diversified indices (like the MSCI ACWI) have annualized roughly 8-8.5%. This simulator defaults to 7.0%, applying an intentional ~20% "margin of safety" haircut to account for future uncertainties and potential lower-growth decades.

Expected Currency Trend & Currency Risk

The MAS uses exchange rate policy to manage inflation, meaning the SGD has historically appreciated against the USD. An "Expected Currency Trend" assumption (e.g., -0.8%) models this depreciation of the US Dollar, gradually reducing the local SGD purchasing power of your global portfolio over decades. For simplicity, the simple mode preset starts at 0%.

⚠️ Limitations & Blindspots (For Beginners)

Real life is messier than math. Do not base major life decisions on this chart without understanding its blindspots:

  • Sequence of Returns Risk (SORR) & Cash Buffers: Because the simulator draws down proportionally, it assumes you continue selling equities even during a market crash. In reality, you should actively manage a cash buffer (using dynamic strategies like the Guyton-Klinger rules) to avoid locking in losses when the market is down.
  • The "Stagflation" Blindspot: The macroeconomic preset buttons pair lower market returns with higher inflation. However, real life can feature "Stagflation" where inflation spikes dramatically at the exact same time markets crash (e.g., 2022). You must manually type in high inflation and negative returns to stress-test this.
  • Invisible Tax Drag: If your global portfolio includes US-domiciled ETFs, the US levies a 30% Dividend Withholding Tax on Singaporeans. A projected 8% gross return might effectively be 7.4% after this tax drag.
  • The Retirement "Smile": The base tool inflates expenses on a straight line. Real retirement spending usually "smiles"—high early on (travel), lower in the middle, and spiking heavily at the end (healthcare). Use Advanced Mode's "Milestone Capital Events" to properly model this.

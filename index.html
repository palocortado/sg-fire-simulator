Assumptions & Beginner Blindspots Guide

🛡️ The CPF LIFE "Margin of Safety"

This simulator deliberately excludes CPF LIFE payouts and the Age 55 Retirement Account (RA) deduction. We recommend treating your eventual CPF LIFE payouts as a pure "margin of safety" rather than relying on it to fund your base FIRE calculations. For conceptual planning, the 2026 CPF cohort benchmarks (in today's dollars, which will automatically inflate for future cohorts) are:

  • Basic Retirement Sum (BRS): $110,200 yielding ~$890 to $930/mo.
  • Full Retirement Sum (FRS): $220,400 yielding ~$1,640 to $1,750/mo.

* Note: These payouts begin at age 65.

Historical Global Returns & Margin of Safety

When projecting global equity returns, history offers a guide. Over the last 20-30 years, US-only indices (like the S&P 500) have annualized roughly 10-10.5%, while globally diversified indices (like the MSCI ACWI) have annualized roughly 8-8.5%. This simulator defaults to 7.0%, applying an intentional ~20% "margin of safety" haircut to account for future uncertainties and potential lower-growth decades.

Expected Currency Trend & Currency Risk

The MAS uses exchange rate policy to manage inflation, meaning the SGD has historically appreciated against the USD. An "Expected Currency Trend" assumption (e.g., -0.8%) models this depreciation of the US Dollar, gradually reducing the local SGD purchasing power of your global portfolio over decades. For simplicity, the preset starts at 0%.

Architecture & Withdrawals in Retirement

When you retire and expenses exceed income, capital must be drawn down. This simulator uses a Proportional Drawdown method, meaning it withdraws from your Cash, SG Equities, and Global Equities concurrently, based on the percentage size of each asset class within your overall portfolio.

Real-World Best Practice (Dynamic Drawdown): In reality, financial planners recommend dynamic strategies like the Guyton-Klinger rules. Instead of proportional selling, you maintain a "cash tent" (1-3 years of expenses). When markets are up, you sell stocks to live and refill the cash. When markets crash, you stop selling stocks entirely and live off the cash buffer until recovery. The proportional drawdown modeled here is a mathematical simplification and does not offer the full protection of a true dynamic cash-tent strategy.

⚠️ Limitations & Blindspots (For Beginners)

Real life is messier than math. Do not base major life decisions on this chart without understanding its blindspots:

  • Sequence of Returns Risk (SORR) & Cash Buffers: Because the simulator draws down proportionally, it assumes you continue selling equities even during a market crash. In reality, you should actively manage a cash buffer (as described above) to avoid locking in losses when the market is down.
  • The "Stagflation" Blindspot: The macroeconomic preset buttons pair lower market returns with higher inflation. However, real life can feature "Stagflation" where inflation spikes dramatically at the exact same time markets crash (e.g., 2022). You must manually type in high inflation and negative returns to stress-test this.
  • Invisible Tax Drag: If your global portfolio includes US-domiciled ETFs, the US levies a 30% Dividend Withholding Tax on Singaporeans. A projected 8% gross return might effectively be 7.4% after this tax drag.
  • The Retirement "Smile": The tool inflates expenses on a straight line. Real retirement spending usually "smiles"—high early on (travel), lower in the middle, and spiking heavily at the end (healthcare).
